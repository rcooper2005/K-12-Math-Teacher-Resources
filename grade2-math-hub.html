üë©‚Äçüè´ Place Value to 1000

Key Concepts for Teachers

The Big Ideas of Place Value
  • Grouping by tens: Our number system groups by tens - 10 ones = 1 ten, 10 tens = 1 hundred
  • Positional notation: The position of a digit determines its value
  • Trading/Regrouping: We can exchange equivalent amounts (10 ones for 1 ten)
  • Unitizing: Treating a group as a single unit (1 hundred is one unit composed of 100 ones)
Second Grade Focus

Students extend understanding from two-digit to three-digit numbers. Key milestones:

  • Understand 100 as "a hundred" (one unit) and as 10 tens and as 100 ones
  • Read and write numbers to 1000 in standard, word, and expanded form
  • Compare numbers using place value understanding
  • Skip count by 5s, 10s, and 100s (foundation for multiplication)

‚ûï Addition & Subtraction Strategies

Strategies for Adding Within 100

Key Strategies
  • Add/subtract tens and ones separately: 45 + 32 = (40+30) + (5+2) = 70 + 7 = 77
  • Compensation: 49 + 23 = 50 + 22 = 72
  • Breaking apart by place value: 67 - 28 = 67 - 20 - 8 = 47 - 8 = 39
  • Adding up (for subtraction): 62 - 45: Start at 45, add 5 to get 50, add 12 to get 62. 5 + 12 = 17
  • Using known facts: If 7 + 8 = 15, then 47 + 8 = 55
Standard Algorithm Readiness

Second grade focuses on place value strategies. The standard algorithm is introduced but understanding comes first. Students should be able to:

  • Explain why regrouping works
  • Use multiple strategies flexibly
  • Check answers using inverse operations

üìö Lesson 1: Understanding Hundreds

‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will understand that 100 is made of 10 tens
  • Students will represent three-digit numbers with base ten blocks
  • Students will write numbers in expanded form

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): "Yesterday we worked with tens and ones. What happens when we get 10 tens?" Count 10 tens together. "10 tens makes 1 hundred!" Show the hundred flat.

Guided Practice (15 min): Build 234 together. "How many hundreds? How many tens? How many ones?" Write in expanded form: 200 + 30 + 4 = 234.

Independent Practice (15 min): Students draw number cards and build with base ten blocks. Record: ___ hundreds + ___ tens + ___ ones = ___.

Closure (5 min): "If I have 4 hundreds, 2 tens, and 6 ones, what number do I have?"

üìö Lesson 2: Adding with Regrouping

‚è±Ô∏è 50 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will add two-digit numbers with regrouping
  • Students will explain why regrouping works using place value
  • Students will connect concrete models to written work

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Present 47 + 35. Build with base ten blocks. "When I add the ones, I get 12 ones. Can I have 12 ones in the ones place?"

Guided Practice (15 min): Demonstrate regrouping: 12 ones = 1 ten and 2 ones. Trade 10 unit cubes for 1 ten rod. "Now I have 8 tens and 2 ones = 82."

Independent Practice (20 min): Students solve problems with blocks, then draw their work, then write the equation.

Closure (5 min): "Why can we trade 10 ones for 1 ten? Does it change the amount?"

üìö Lesson 3: Measuring in Inches and Centimeters

‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will measure objects to the nearest inch and centimeter
  • Students will understand that the same object has different measurements in different units
  • Students will line up rulers correctly starting at 0

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Review ruler parts. "Where do we start measuring? At 0, not at the edge!" Model measuring a pencil in inches.

Guided Practice (10 min): Measure the same pencil in centimeters. "Is the pencil a different length? No! Just different units."

Independent Practice (20 min): Measurement scavenger hunt - measure 8 objects in both inches and centimeters. Record in a table.

Closure (5 min): "Which number is always bigger - inches or centimeters? Why?"

üìö Lesson 4: Telling Time to Five Minutes

‚è±Ô∏è 40 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will tell time to the nearest five minutes
  • Students will connect skip counting by 5s to minutes
  • Students will write time in digital format

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Review hour and half-hour. "The minute hand moves around the clock. Let's skip count by 5s as it moves: 5, 10, 15, 20..."

Guided Practice (15 min): Show various times. Students identify hour (short hand) and minutes (skip count to where long hand points). Practice writing as 3:25.

Independent Practice (10 min): Time matching - match analog clocks to digital times.

Closure (5 min): "Show me 4:35 on your clock."

üìö Lesson 5: Introduction to Arrays

‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will arrange objects into rectangular arrays
  • Students will write repeated addition equations for arrays
  • Students will find the total objects in an array

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Show 12 square tiles. "How can we arrange these in equal rows?" Create a 3√ó4 array. "I have 3 rows with 4 in each row."

Guided Practice (15 min): Write equation: 4 + 4 + 4 = 12. "What other arrays can we make with 12?" Try 2√ó6, 4√ó3, 6√ó2, 1√ó12.

Independent Practice (15 min): Students build arrays for 18 tiles. Find all possible arrays. Write repeated addition for each.

Closure (5 min): "How do arrays help us add equal groups?"

üî¨ Investigation 1: The Trading Game

Focus: Place value and regrouping | Duration: 40 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will understand regrouping as trading equivalent amounts
  • Students will fluently exchange 10 ones for 1 ten, 10 tens for 1 hundred

Procedure

  1. Partners take turns rolling dice and taking that many unit cubes
  2. Whenever you have 10 of a unit, you MUST trade for the next larger unit
  3. First to reach 1 hundred (flat) wins!
  4. Record your number after each turn

Discussion

"Why did we trade 10 ones for 1 ten? Did we change how much we had?"

üî¨ Investigation 2: Measurement Olympics

Focus: Measuring and comparing lengths | Duration: 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will measure distances in inches, feet, and centimeters
  • Students will compare measurements and find differences

Procedure

  1. Set up "events": standing long jump, ball throw, paper airplane flight
  2. Students measure their results in inches and centimeters
  3. Record all measurements in a class chart
  4. Calculate: How much farther did ____ jump than ____?
  5. Create a line plot of the data

üî¨ Investigation 3: Odd or Even?

Focus: Properties of odd and even numbers | Duration: 35 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will determine if numbers are odd or even
  • Students will discover patterns in odd and even numbers

Procedure

  1. Give students linking cubes. "Can you pair them all up perfectly, or is there one left over?"
  2. Test numbers 1-20. Record which are odd (leftover) and even (no leftover)
  3. Discover pattern: Even numbers end in 0, 2, 4, 6, 8
  4. Investigate: What happens when you add two even numbers? Two odd numbers? One of each?

üî¨ Investigation 4: Partition Party

Focus: Equal shares/fractions | Duration: 40 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will partition shapes into 2, 3, and 4 equal parts
  • Students will name parts as halves, thirds, and fourths

Procedure

  1. Problem: "We have one pizza. How can 2 people share it equally? 3 people? 4 people?"
  2. Students fold paper circles into halves, thirds, fourths
  3. Explore rectangles: How many ways can you divide a rectangle into fourths?
  4. Key question: "Which is bigger - a half or a fourth? Why?"

üìñ Student Reading Passages

üìö What is Place Value?

Reading Level: 2nd Grade | Topic: Place value to 1000

In math, where a number sits matters! This is called place value.

In the number 352, each digit has a different value. The 3 is in the hundreds place. It means 300! The 5 is in the tens place. It means 50. The 2 is in the ones place. It means just 2.

So 352 = 300 + 50 + 2. We call this expanded form!

üìö Measuring with Rulers

Reading Level: 2nd Grade | Topic: Standard measurement

A ruler helps us measure how long things are. But you have to use it the right way!

First, find the zero. Line up your object with zero, not with the edge of the ruler. Then look at the other end of your object. What number is it closest to?

We measure in inches or centimeters. The same pencil might be 6 inches OR 15 centimeters. It's not longer - just different units!

üìö Odd and Even Numbers

Reading Level: 2nd Grade | Topic: Number properties

Some numbers are EVEN. Some are ODD. How can you tell?

Even numbers can be split into two equal groups. If you have 8 cookies, you can give 4 to your friend and keep 4. No leftovers!

Odd numbers always have one left over. If you have 7 cookies, you can give 3 away and keep 3, but there's 1 left!

Easy trick: Even numbers end in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8. Odd numbers end in 1, 3, 5, 7, or 9.

üìñ Math Vocabulary

Hundreds

Definition: The place value representing groups of 100

"In 456, the 4 is in the hundreds place and represents 400."

Expanded Form

Definition: Writing a number as the sum of each digit's value

"352 in expanded form is 300 + 50 + 2."

Regroup

Definition: To trade 10 of one unit for 1 of the next larger unit

"When adding 47 + 35, we regroup 10 ones into 1 ten."

Even

Definition: A number that can be divided into 2 equal groups with nothing left over

"8 is even because 4 + 4 = 8."

Odd

Definition: A number that has 1 left over when divided into 2 groups

"7 is odd because 3 + 3 + 1 = 7."

Array

Definition: Objects arranged in equal rows and columns

"A 3√ó4 array has 3 rows and 4 columns, totaling 12."

Third

Definition: One of three equal parts

"If we share a pizza among 3 friends, each gets one third."

Estimate

Definition: To make a reasonable guess about a measurement or answer

"I estimate this pencil is about 6 inches long."

‚ùì Common Misconceptions

‚ùå The larger digit means the larger number

Example: Student thinks 92 > 124 because 9 > 1.

Address: Compare place by place starting from the left. Build both with base ten blocks to see which has more.

‚ùå When regrouping, the 1 represents "one"

Example: In 47 + 35, student writes a "1" but thinks it's 1, not 10.

Address: Always connect to physical trading. "We traded 10 ones for 1 TEN."

‚ùå Measuring from the edge of the ruler instead of 0

Example: Student lines up object with ruler edge, getting incorrect measurement.

Address: Use "broken rulers" to practice finding 0. Always ask "Where should we start?"

‚ùå More pieces means more total amount (fractions)

Example: Student thinks 4 fourths is more than 2 halves.

Address: Compare using same-size wholes. Fold paper to show halves and fourths of the same shape.
