Problem Solving

Build new mathematical knowledge through problem solving: Students solve problems involving counting objects, comparing quantities, and identifying shapes in their environment.

Reasoning & Proof

Make and investigate mathematical conjectures: Students explain why they think one group has more or fewer objects and justify their counting strategies.

üë©‚Äçüè´ Counting & Cardinality Background

Key Concepts for Teachers

The Counting Principles
  • Stable Order: Number words are always said in the same order (one, two, three...)
  • One-to-One Correspondence: Each object is counted exactly once with one number word
  • Cardinality: The last number said tells how many objects in the set
  • Abstraction: Any collection of objects can be counted
  • Order Irrelevance: Objects can be counted in any order; the total remains the same
Developmental Progression

Children typically progress through these stages:

  • Rote Counting: Saying numbers in order without objects
  • Counting Objects: Matching number words to objects
  • Counting with Cardinality: Understanding the last number represents the total
  • Counting On: Starting from a given number rather than 1
  • Subitizing: Instantly recognizing small quantities (1-5) without counting
Common Student Questions
  • "Why do we start at one?" - Discuss zero as a starting point representing "none"
  • "What comes after 100?" - Build excitement about larger numbers
  • "Why is this called a square and a rectangle?" - Squares are special rectangles

‚ûï Operations Background (Addition & Subtraction)

Understanding Early Operations

Problem Types (Cognitively Guided Instruction)
  • Join (Add To): Start with some, add more (Result Unknown, Change Unknown, Start Unknown)
  • Separate (Take From): Start with some, take away (Result Unknown, Change Unknown, Start Unknown)
  • Part-Part-Whole: Two parts make a whole (Whole Unknown, Part Unknown)
  • Compare: Comparing two quantities (Difference Unknown, Bigger Unknown, Smaller Unknown)
Strategies Children Use
  • Direct Modeling: Using objects to act out the problem
  • Counting All: Counting both groups from 1
  • Counting On: Starting from one number and counting up
  • Derived Facts: Using known facts to find unknown ones
  • Recall: Knowing facts from memory

üî∑ Geometry Background

Shape Understanding

Van Hiele Levels of Geometric Thinking
  • Level 0 - Visualization: Recognize shapes by appearance (Kindergarteners are here)
  • Level 1 - Analysis: Identify properties of shapes
  • Level 2 - Informal Deduction: Understand relationships between shapes

At Kindergarten, children recognize shapes holistically. A triangle is a triangle because it "looks like" one, not because it has three sides.

2D Shapes for Kindergarten
  • Circle: Round, curved line, no corners or sides
  • Triangle: 3 sides, 3 corners (vertices)
  • Square: 4 equal sides, 4 square corners
  • Rectangle: 4 sides, 4 square corners, opposite sides equal
  • Hexagon: 6 sides, 6 corners
3D Shapes for Kindergarten
  • Sphere: Ball shape, no flat surfaces
  • Cube: Box shape, 6 square faces
  • Cylinder: Can shape, 2 circular faces, curved surface
  • Cone: Ice cream cone shape, 1 circular face, pointed top

üìè Measurement Background

Early Measurement Concepts

Direct Comparison

In Kindergarten, students compare objects directly without using measurement units.

  • Length: Longer, shorter, taller, same length
  • Weight: Heavier, lighter (using balance scales)
  • Capacity: Holds more, holds less
Teaching Tips
  • Always align objects at a common baseline when comparing length
  • Use familiar objects for comparison (pencils, shoes, books)
  • Introduce measurement vocabulary in context
  • Connect to sorting and classification activities

üìö Lesson 1: Counting Collections

‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will count a collection of objects accurately using one-to-one correspondence
  • Students will demonstrate cardinality by stating how many objects are in a collection
  • Students will organize objects to make counting easier

Materials Needed

  • Collections of small objects (buttons, shells, pasta, beads) - 10-20 per student
  • Counting mats or small trays
  • Recording sheets
  • Crayons or markers

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Gather students and show a collection of objects. Ask "How can we find out how many?" Model counting with one-to-one correspondence, moving each object as you count.

Guided Practice (10 min): Give pairs a small collection. Have them count together, watching for students who skip objects or double-count. Ask "How many do you have?" to check cardinality.

Independent Practice (20 min): Students work with individual collections. Encourage organizing (lines, groups, circles) to keep track. Students record their count by drawing circles and writing the numeral.

Closure (5 min): Students share strategies. "How did you keep track?" Highlight organizing strategies.

Assessment

Observe: Does the student touch each object once? Say numbers in correct order? Know the last number is the total? Organize objects?

üìö Lesson 2: Ten Frame Fun

‚è±Ô∏è 40 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will place counters on a ten frame using the top row first, left to right
  • Students will identify quantities 1-10 on a ten frame
  • Students will begin to subitize quantities within 5

Materials Needed

  • Large demonstration ten frame
  • Student ten frames (laminated or on paper)
  • Two-color counters
  • Ten frame dot cards

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (8 min): Introduce the ten frame. "This special frame has 10 boxes - 5 on top, 5 on bottom." Model filling top row first, left to right. "Why might we fill it this way?"

Guided Practice (12 min): Flash ten frame cards. "How many? How do you know?" Practice "seeing" 5 as a full row. Show various arrangements (3, 5, 7, 10).

Independent Practice (15 min): Students practice filling ten frames to different amounts. Partner activity: one student calls a number, other fills the frame.

Closure (5 min): Quick flash assessment with cards. Discuss: "What helps you know how many quickly?"

üìö Lesson 3: Shape Detectives

‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will identify circles, triangles, squares, and rectangles
  • Students will describe shapes using informal language (sides, corners, round)
  • Students will find shapes in their environment

Materials Needed

  • Large shape cutouts
  • Pattern blocks
  • Shape detective recording sheet
  • Camera or tablet (optional)
  • Book: "The Shape of Things" by Dayle Ann Dodds

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Read "The Shape of Things." Introduce each shape with large cutouts. Students trace shapes in the air and describe what they notice.

Guided Practice (10 min): Sort pattern blocks by shape. Students describe: "How did you know this was a triangle?" Introduce vocabulary (sides, corners).

Independent Practice (20 min): Shape Hunt! Students become "Shape Detectives" and find shapes in the classroom. Record on sheet by drawing the object and naming the shape.

Closure (5 min): Share discoveries. Create class chart of shapes found in our room.

üìö Lesson 4: More, Less, or Equal

‚è±Ô∏è 40 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will compare two groups of objects using "more than," "less than," and "equal to"
  • Students will use matching to determine which group has more
  • Students will explain their reasoning using math vocabulary

Materials Needed

  • Two-color counters
  • Comparison mats with two sections
  • Number cards 1-10
  • Linking cubes

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (8 min): Show two groups of objects (7 and 4). "Which group has more? How can we find out without counting?" Introduce matching strategy - line up objects one-to-one.

Guided Practice (12 min): Practice with linking cube towers. Build two towers and stand them next to each other. Introduce vocabulary: "7 is more than 4" and "4 is less than 7."

Independent Practice (15 min): Partners draw number cards and build groups. Compare using matching. Record: "__ is more than __" or "__ is less than __" or "__ is equal to __".

Closure (5 min): Play "Stand Up If..." game. "Stand up if you have more than 5 letters in your name."

üìö Lesson 5: Addition Stories

‚è±Ô∏è 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will act out addition story problems
  • Students will represent addition with objects and drawings
  • Students will understand that addition means putting groups together

Materials Needed

  • Counters
  • Story problem cards
  • Part-part-whole mats
  • Drawing paper
  • Crayons

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (10 min): Tell a story: "2 birds were sitting in a tree. 3 more birds flew in. How many birds are in the tree now?" Have students act it out. Count to verify.

Guided Practice (10 min): Model with counters on part-part-whole mat. Show drawing strategy: draw circles for each group, count all. Introduce + and = symbols.

Independent Practice (20 min): Students solve addition stories using objects, drawings, or both. Stories include: playground scenarios, animal stories, food problems.

Closure (5 min): Students share one story and solution. Connect to equation: 2 + 3 = 5.

üî¨ Investigation 1: How Many Are Hiding?

Focus: Decomposing numbers within 10 | Duration: 30 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will decompose numbers up to 10 into two parts
  • Students will develop number sense by finding missing parts
  • Students will record different ways to break apart numbers

Materials

  • Counters or small objects (5-10 per student)
  • Cups or containers for hiding
  • Recording sheet

Procedure

  1. Partners work with 5 counters total. One partner hides some under a cup.
  2. Other partner sees the remaining counters and figures out how many are hiding.
  3. Check by lifting the cup. Record: "3 and 2 make 5"
  4. Switch roles and repeat with different amounts hidden.
  5. Extension: Increase to 10 counters for students who are ready.

Discussion Questions

  • How did you figure out how many were hiding?
  • What strategies helped you?
  • How many different ways can you break apart 5?

üî¨ Investigation 2: Shape Building Challenge

Focus: Composing shapes | Duration: 35 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will compose larger shapes from smaller shapes
  • Students will discover relationships between shapes
  • Students will develop spatial reasoning skills

Materials

  • Pattern blocks
  • Hexagon outline mats
  • Recording sheet

Procedure

  1. Challenge: "How many different ways can you fill this hexagon?"
  2. Students use pattern blocks to fill the hexagon outline.
  3. Record each solution by tracing or drawing the blocks used.
  4. Compare solutions: "Did anyone use different shapes?"
  5. Extension: Create your own design using exactly 10 blocks.

Discussion Questions

  • How many triangles does it take to fill the hexagon?
  • Which shapes work well together?
  • What did you discover about how shapes fit together?

üî¨ Investigation 3: Measurement Scavenger Hunt

Focus: Comparing lengths | Duration: 40 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will compare objects by length using direct comparison
  • Students will use vocabulary: longer, shorter, taller, same length
  • Students will order objects from shortest to longest

Materials

  • Reference object (paper strip, craft stick)
  • Recording sheet with categories: Longer Than, Shorter Than, Same As
  • Various classroom objects

Procedure

  1. Give each student a reference strip. "Find things that are longer, shorter, or the same length."
  2. Students hunt for objects and compare directly by lining up ends.
  3. Record findings by drawing or writing object names.
  4. Return to group and share: sort actual objects by length.
  5. Order 5 objects from shortest to longest.

Discussion Questions

  • How did you make sure your comparison was fair?
  • What was the longest thing you found? Shortest?
  • Were any objects tricky to compare? Why?

üî¨ Investigation 4: Sort It Out

Focus: Classification and data | Duration: 35 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will sort objects by one attribute (color, shape, size)
  • Students will count objects in each category
  • Students will compare groups using "more" and "fewer"

Materials

  • Collection of buttons (varying colors, sizes, shapes, number of holes)
  • Sorting mats
  • Graphing mat

Procedure

  1. Partners receive button collection. "Sort these buttons. You decide how!"
  2. After sorting, students explain their rule: "We sorted by ___."
  3. Count buttons in each group and record numbers.
  4. Compare groups: Which has more? Fewer? How many more?
  5. Re-sort using a different rule.

Discussion Questions

  • What rule did you use to sort?
  • Did any buttons fit in more than one group?
  • How could you sort the same buttons a different way?

üî¨ Investigation 5: The Numberless Number Line

Focus: Number sequence and order | Duration: 30 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will understand number order and sequence
  • Students will place numbers in correct position relative to others
  • Students will develop understanding of number magnitude

Materials

  • Floor number line (tape or rope)
  • Number cards 0-20
  • Clothespins or clips

Procedure

  1. Create blank number line on floor. Place only 0 at start and 10 at end.
  2. Give students number cards. "Where does your number belong?"
  3. Students place numbers, explaining reasoning: "5 goes in the middle because..."
  4. Check spacing: Are numbers evenly spaced?
  5. Extension: Remove some numbers and have others fill in missing spots.

Discussion Questions

  • How did you know where to place your number?
  • What numbers helped you figure it out?
  • Which number is closest to 10? To 0?

üìù Assessment Tools

üéØ Counting Assessment (One-on-One)

Rote Counting: "Count as high as you can." (Record highest number)

Counting Objects: Give student 20 objects. "Count these for me." (Note: 1-1 correspondence, organization, cardinality)

Counting On: "Start at 7 and keep counting." (Can they start from any number?)

Count Backwards: "Count backwards from 10." (Important for subtraction readiness)

Scoring Guide:

  • Exceeds: Counts to 100+, counts on fluently, understands cardinality
  • Meets: Counts to 20 accurately with 1-1 correspondence
  • Approaching: Counts to 10 with some errors in 1-1 correspondence
  • Beginning: Counts to 5 or less, does not demonstrate cardinality

üéØ Number Recognition Assessment

Materials: Number cards 0-20 in random order

Procedure: Show each card. "What number is this?"

Record: Circle numbers student identifies correctly

Extension: "Write the number ___" (numeral formation)

üéØ Shape Identification Assessment

2D Shapes: Show shapes in different sizes and orientations. "What shape is this?"

Shapes to assess: Circle, square, rectangle, triangle, hexagon

3D Shapes: Show solid figures. "What shape is this?"

Shapes to assess: Sphere, cube, cylinder, cone

Extension: "Tell me about this shape" (sides, corners)

üéØ Addition/Subtraction Assessment

With Manipulatives: "Show me 3 + 2 with these counters. How many altogether?"

Story Problems: "There are 4 birds. 2 more come. How many birds now?"

Within 5 Facts: Flash cards for fluency within 5

Observe student strategies:

  • Direct modeling (acts out with objects)
  • Counting all (counts both groups from 1)
  • Counting on (starts from larger number)
  • Known facts (recalls from memory)

üìñ Math Vocabulary

Count

Definition: To say numbers in order while matching each number to one object

"Let's count the apples: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. There are 5 apples!"

Number

Definition: A symbol that tells how many (0, 1, 2, 3...)

"The number 7 tells us there are seven objects."

More

Definition: A greater amount or number

"8 is more than 5. The red group has more."

Less/Fewer

Definition: A smaller amount or number

"3 is less than 7. The blue group has fewer."

Equal/Same

Definition: The same amount or number

"5 and 5 are equal. Both groups have the same."

Add/Plus

Definition: To put groups together to find how many in all

"When we add 2 and 3, we get 5."

Subtract/Take Away

Definition: To take some away from a group

"If we take away 2 from 5, we have 3 left."

Circle

Definition: A round shape with no corners or sides

"A wheel is shaped like a circle."

Triangle

Definition: A shape with 3 straight sides and 3 corners

"A yield sign is a triangle."

Square

Definition: A shape with 4 equal sides and 4 square corners

"A checkerboard has squares."

Rectangle

Definition: A shape with 4 sides and 4 square corners; opposite sides are equal

"A door is shaped like a rectangle."

Side

Definition: A straight line that is part of a shape

"A triangle has 3 sides."

Corner (Vertex)

Definition: A point where two sides meet

"A square has 4 corners."

Longer

Definition: Greater in length

"The pencil is longer than the crayon."

Shorter

Definition: Less in length

"The crayon is shorter than the pencil."

Sort

Definition: To put objects into groups by how they are alike

"Let's sort the shapes by color."

Pattern

Definition: Something that repeats in a regular way

"Red, blue, red, blue is a pattern!"

Ten Frame

Definition: A frame with 10 boxes to help us count and see numbers

"Put 7 counters on the ten frame."

üéÆ Math Games & Activities

üé≤ Roll and Count

Players: 2-4 | Materials: Dice, counters, ten frame

  • Roll the die and place that many counters on your ten frame
  • Keep rolling and adding until your ten frame is full
  • First to fill the frame wins!
  • Variation: Use two dice for numbers to 12; use a 20-frame

üéØ Number Showdown

Players: 2 | Materials: Number cards 0-10

  • Split the deck between two players
  • Both flip a card at the same time
  • The player with the larger number takes both cards
  • If tied, flip again - winner takes all four
  • Variation: Smaller number wins; add flipped numbers together

üî∑ Shape Hunt Bingo

Players: Whole class | Materials: Bingo cards with shapes, markers

  • Students have bingo cards with various 2D and 3D shapes
  • Teacher calls out a shape or shows a picture of a real object
  • Students mark the matching shape on their card
  • First to get 4 in a row calls "BINGO!"

üêª Bear Cave (How Many Are Hiding?)

Players: 2 | Materials: Small bears or counters, cup

  • Start with a known number of bears (5-10)
  • One player hides some bears under the cup (cave)
  • Other player counts remaining bears and guesses how many are hiding
  • Check by lifting the cup
  • Focus: Decomposing numbers, subtraction foundations

üö∂ Number Line Hop

Players: 2-4 | Materials: Floor number line, dice or spinner

  • All players start at 0
  • Roll die and hop that many spaces forward
  • Say each number as you hop
  • First to reach 10 (or 20) wins!
  • Variation: Add spinner for +1, +2, -1 to practice addition and subtraction

üÉè Make 5/Make 10

Players: 2-4 | Materials: Number cards 0-5 (or 0-10)

  • Spread cards face up in middle
  • Players take turns finding two cards that make 5 (or 10)
  • Take matching pairs; replace from deck
  • Most pairs at end wins!
  • Focus: Number bonds, addition facts

üåü Differentiation Strategies

üìà For Advanced Learners

  • Extend number range: Count to higher numbers, work with numbers 20-100

  • Explore patterns: Introduce skip counting by 2s, 5s, and 10s
  • Shape challenges: Explore more complex shapes, symmetry, and tessellations
  • Number relationships: Explore odd/even, greater than/less than with larger numbers
  • Leadership roles: Peer tutoring opportunities

üìâ For Struggling Learners

  • Reduce number range: Focus on numbers 0-10 before extending
  • Concrete manipulatives: Always use objects before pictures before symbols
  • One-to-one support: More frequent check-ins and guided practice
  • Visual supports: Number lines, ten frames always available
  • Chunked instruction: Break tasks into smaller steps
  • Extra counting practice: Daily counting routines with movement
  • Modified recording: Stamps, stickers, or drawing instead of writing numerals

üåê For English Language Learners

  • Visual vocabulary: Picture cards for all math terms
  • Gestures and movements: Connect numbers to hand signals
  • Native language support: Number words in home language alongside English
  • Manipulative-based instruction: Less language, more doing
  • Partner with bilingual peers: Collaborative learning opportunities
  • Real object connections: Use culturally familiar objects for counting
  • Sentence frames: "I have ___ (number)." "This shape is a ___."

üéØ For Students with IEP/504 Plans

  • Extended time: Allow more time for counting and problem-solving
  • Reduced quantity: Fewer problems with same concepts

  • Visual schedules: Clear expectations for math routines

  • Assistive technology: Apps for number practice, voice recording

  • Choice boards: Students select activities at their level
  • Station rotation: Teacher-led small group, independent practice, games

‚ùì Common Misconceptions

‚ùå Misconception: The last number counted is just a label, not the total

What students think: When counting 5 objects, "5" is just what they call the last object, not how many total.

Why it happens: Cardinality (the last number represents the total) is a developmental milestone, not automatic.

How to address: After counting, always ask "How many?" If student recounts, they haven't grasped cardinality yet. Use the "magic move" - dramatically sweep hand over all objects while stating the total. Practice with ten frames where the arrangement helps students see the total.

‚ùå Misconception: Counting means touching while saying numbers, even if items are skipped or double-counted

What students think: As long as I'm saying numbers and touching things, I'm counting correctly.

Why it happens: One-to-one correspondence is still developing; motor control and verbal sequencing are challenging to coordinate.

How to address: Slow down! Model moving objects to a new location as each is counted. Use larger objects that are easier to track. Arrange objects in a line before counting. Have students "catch" counters in a cup as they count.

‚ùå Misconception: A shape isn't a triangle if it's oriented differently

What students think: Triangles must have a flat bottom and point at the top. Upside-down or tilted triangles aren't "real" triangles.

Why it happens: Students at the visual level of geometric thinking recognize shapes by overall appearance, not properties.

How to address: Show many examples of triangles in different orientations. Ask "Does it still have 3 sides? 3 corners?" Rotate shapes together and discuss if properties change. Include varied orientations in all shape activities.

‚ùå Misconception: The longer row has more, regardless of actual quantity

What students think: If one row of counters is spread out more, it has more objects than a shorter, tighter row.

Why it happens: This relates to Piaget's concept of conservation - understanding that quantity remains the same despite changes in appearance.

How to address: Always count to verify comparisons. Use matching (lining up objects one-to-one) as a comparison strategy. After spreading out a row, ask "Did we add any? Take any away?" to build conservation understanding.

‚ùå Misconception: The equal sign means "the answer comes next"

What students think: In 2 + 3 = __, the = symbol means "write your answer here" rather than representing balance or equivalence.

Why it happens: Students are typically exposed only to equations with the answer position on the right.

How to address: Use balance scales to show equivalence physically. Present equations in multiple formats: 5 = 2 + 3, 2 + __ = 5, __ = 3 + 2. Emphasize "equals means the same as" language.

‚ùå Misconception: Addition always makes bigger, subtraction always makes smaller

What students think: If I add, I always get a bigger number. If I subtract, I always get a smaller number.

Why it happens: At this level, students haven't encountered zero or negative contexts yet, so this seems logical.

How to address: Introduce adding zero: "3 + 0 = 3. Did it get bigger?" and subtracting zero: "5 - 0 = 5. Did it get smaller?" This is the perfect level to begin building accurate conceptual foundations.

‚ùå Misconception: "Teen" numbers are just random new numbers

What students think: 11, 12, 13... are completely new numbers with no connection to 1, 2, 3...

Why it happens: English number words (eleven, twelve, thirteen) don't clearly express the "10 and some more" structure the way some other languages do.

How to address: Use double ten frames to show 10 and some more. Build teen numbers with Unifix cubes: a stick of 10 plus extras. Explicitly teach "13 is 10 and 3 more" while building representations. Connect to place value concepts.

‚ùå Misconception: Squares aren't rectangles

What students think: Squares and rectangles are completely different shapes with nothing in common.

Why it happens: We often teach shapes as separate categories without discussing relationships.

How to address: While we don't need to formally teach squares as special rectangles in K, we can lay groundwork: "Squares have 4 sides and 4 corners, just like rectangles. Squares are special because all 4 sides are the same!" Avoid saying "that's not a rectangle, it's a square."
