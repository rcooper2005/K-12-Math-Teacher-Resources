üìä Exponential Functions

Growth and Decay

General Form: y = a ¬∑ bÀ£
  • a: Initial value (y-intercept when x = 0)
  • b: Growth factor (b > 1) or decay factor (0 < b < 1)
Linear vs. Exponential

Linear: Add constant amount each time (arithmetic sequence)

Exponential: Multiply by constant factor each time (geometric sequence)

Applications
  • Compound interest: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
  • Population growth: P = P‚ÇÄ ¬∑ (1 + r)^t
  • Radioactive decay: A = A‚ÇÄ ¬∑ (0.5)^(t/half-life)

üîÄ Systems of Equations

Finding Where Functions Meet

Solution Methods
  • Graphing: Find intersection point visually
  • Substitution: Solve one equation for a variable, substitute into other
  • Elimination: Add/subtract equations to eliminate a variable
Types of Solutions
  • One solution: Lines intersect at one point
  • No solution: Parallel lines (inconsistent)
  • Infinite solutions: Same line (dependent)

üìö Lesson 1: Writing Linear Equations from Context

‚è±Ô∏è 55 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will identify slope and y-intercept from word problems
  • Students will write equations in slope-intercept form
  • Students will interpret equations in context

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (12 min): "A gym charges $50 enrollment plus $30 per month. Write an equation for total cost after m months." Identify: starting value ($50 = b) and rate of change ($30/month = m). Equation: C = 30m + 50.

Guided Practice (18 min): Practice with various contexts. Identify what each number represents. Connect to graph interpretation.

Independent Practice (20 min): Write equations from contexts; given equation, describe scenario.

Closure (5 min): "What does the y-intercept represent in real-world problems?"

üìö Lesson 2: Solving Systems by Substitution

‚è±Ô∏è 55 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will solve systems of equations using substitution
  • Students will verify solutions
  • Students will apply to word problems

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (12 min): System: y = 2x + 1 and 3x + y = 11. Since we know y = 2x + 1, replace y in second equation: 3x + (2x + 1) = 11.

Guided Practice (18 min): Solve: 5x + 1 = 11, so x = 2. Then y = 2(2) + 1 = 5. Solution: (2, 5). Verify in BOTH equations!

Independent Practice (20 min): Solve systems; application problems ("Two numbers sum to 20...").

Closure (5 min): "When is substitution the easiest method to use?"

üìö Lesson 3: Factoring Quadratics (x¬≤ + bx + c)

‚è±Ô∏è 55 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will factor trinomials where a = 1
  • Students will find factors that multiply to c and add to b
  • Students will verify by multiplication

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (12 min): Reverse of FOIL. Factor x¬≤ + 7x + 12. Need two numbers that multiply to 12 AND add to 7. List factor pairs of 12: (1,12), (2,6), (3,4). Which adds to 7? 3 + 4 = 7! So x¬≤ + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4).

Guided Practice (18 min): Practice finding factor pairs. Handle negative middle terms. Verify by multiplying back out.

Independent Practice (20 min): Factor various trinomials; identify unfactorable examples.

Closure (5 min): "How do you know if a trinomial can be factored?"

üìö Lesson 4: The Quadratic Formula

‚è±Ô∏è 55 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Students will apply the quadratic formula
  • Students will interpret the discriminant
  • Students will know when to use each solving method

Lesson Procedure

Introduction (12 min): "What if we can't factor?" Introduce formula: x = (-b ¬± ‚àö(b¬≤ - 4ac)) / 2a. Identify a, b, c from standard form.

Guided Practice (18 min): Solve 2x¬≤ + 5x - 3 = 0. a=2, b=5, c=-3. Calculate step by step. Discriminant (b¬≤-4ac) tells number of solutions: positive = 2, zero = 1, negative = 0 real solutions.

üìö Why Algebra Matters

Algebra is the language of patterns and relationships. When you write y = mx + b, you're describing every possible straight line - infinite possibilities in one simple equation!

From predicting profits to designing video games, algebra underlies countless real-world applications. Engineers use systems of equations to balance chemical reactions. Economists use linear models to forecast trends. Scientists use exponential functions to model population growth.

üìö The Beauty of Quadratics

Throw a ball in the air - it follows a parabola! This U-shaped curve appears everywhere: suspension bridges, satellite dishes, and even the path of water from a fountain.

The quadratic formula, discovered over 4,000 years ago by Babylonian mathematicians, gives us the power to find where any parabola crosses the x-axis. That's 4,000 years of human knowledge packed into one formula!
