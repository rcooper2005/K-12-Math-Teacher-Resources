üìê Pre-Calculus Content Standards

Functions

Analysis: Analyze functions using different representations (algebraic, graphical, numerical, verbal).

Building: Build new functions from existing functions (transformations, compositions, inverses).

Types: Polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, piecewise.

Trigonometry

Unit Circle: Extend the domain of trig functions using the unit circle.

Modeling: Model periodic phenomena with trigonometric functions.

Identities: Prove and apply trigonometric identities (Pythagorean, sum/difference, double/half angle).

Inverse Trig: Understand inverse trig functions and their domains/ranges.

Laws: Apply Law of Sines and Law of Cosines to non-right triangles.

Polar & Parametric

Polar: Convert between rectangular and polar coordinates; graph polar equations.

Complex: Represent complex numbers in polar form; DeMoivre's Theorem.

Parametric: Graph parametric equations; eliminate the parameter.

Vectors

Operations: Add, subtract, and scale vectors; find magnitude and direction.

Dot Product: Calculate and interpret dot product; find angle between vectors.

Applications: Work, force, and motion problems.

Sequences & Series

Arithmetic: Find nth term and sum of arithmetic sequences.

Geometric: Find nth term and sum (finite and infinite) of geometric sequences.

Notation: Use sigma notation; expand and evaluate sums.

Introduction to Calculus Concepts

Limits: Understand the concept of limit; evaluate limits graphically and algebraically.

Continuity: Understand continuity and identify discontinuities.

Rates: Average vs. instantaneous rate of change (intuitive).

üìö The Golden Ratio: Mathematics of Beauty

The ratio (1 + ‚àö5)/2 ‚âà 1.618... appears everywhere: in the spiral of a nautilus shell, the arrangement of seeds in a sunflower, and the proportions of the Parthenon.

üìö From Finite to Infinite: The Birth of Calculus

For centuries, mathematicians struggled with infinity. How can you add infinitely many numbers and get a finite answer? The concept of limits, developed by Newton and Leibniz in the 1600s, finally made sense of this.

A limit captures what happens as we approach a value, even if we never quite reach it. This idea is the foundation of calculus, which revolutionized science and engineering.
